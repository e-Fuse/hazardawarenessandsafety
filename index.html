Section 1 — Why It Matters

Workplace injuries are preventable

Every worker has the right to go home safe. But thousands of Australians are seriously injured at work each year — most from hazards that could have been identified and controlled.

497

Workers killed at work in Australia in 2023

Safe Work Australia data shows fatalities are concentrated in construction, agriculture, transport and manufacturing — but no workplace is entirely risk-free. Behind every statistic is a person, a family, and a workplace forever changed.

⚖️

It's the law

The Work Health and Safety Act 2011 gives both employers and workers legally enforceable duties. Breaches can result in serious fines — or prosecution.

💰

The cost is enormous

Workplace injuries cost Australian businesses over $28 billion annually — including compensation claims, lost productivity, and lasting reputational damage.

🤝

Everyone's responsibility

WHS is not just an HR concern. Every worker has a duty to care for their own safety — and the safety of those around them.

ℹ️
State and territory variations: While most Australian states and territories have adopted the Model WHS Act, Victoria, Western Australia, and some others operate under their own WHS legislation. The core duties are consistent — always check the specific regulator for your state if you are unsure.

Section 2 — Hazard Types

Recognising hazards in your workplace

A hazard is anything with the potential to cause harm. Hazards come in many forms — some obvious, some hidden. Select each category to learn more.

🔧 Physical Hazards

The most visible category — anything in the environment that can cause physical harm.

  • ✓Slips, trips and falls — wet floors, uneven surfaces, poor lighting
  • ✓Working at heights without fall protection
  • ✓Noise above safe limits (85dB over 8 hours)
  • ✓Extreme heat or cold exposure
  • ✓Moving machinery, vehicles, or unguarded equipment
  • ✓Electrical hazards from faulty equipment or wiring

⚗️ Chemical Hazards

Any substance that can cause harm through inhalation, skin contact, ingestion, or explosion. Always check the Safety Data Sheet (SDS).

  • ✓Cleaning products, solvents, disinfectants
  • ✓Dusts — silica, wood, and asbestos (in older buildings)
  • ✓Fumes from welding, painting, or industrial processes
  • ✓Pesticides and herbicides in agricultural settings
  • ✓Flammable or corrosive substances stored incorrectly
⚠️
Asbestos: Buildings built before 1987 in Australia may contain asbestos. Never disturb suspected materials — report immediately to your manager.

🦠 Biological Hazards

Exposure to living organisms or their byproducts that can cause infection or illness.

  • ✓Bloodborne pathogens — relevant in healthcare and first aid roles
  • ✓Mould and bacteria in poorly ventilated or water-damaged spaces
  • ✓Animal handling in agriculture, veterinary, or research settings
  • ✓Food handling risks in hospitality and food processing
  • ✓Insects and vermin in outdoor or field-based work

🪑 Ergonomic Hazards

Cause musculoskeletal harm through how tasks, tools, or workstations are designed. Often underreported because damage builds gradually.

  • ✓Repetitive movements — fine motor tasks performed for hours
  • ✓Manual handling — lifting, pushing, or carrying heavy loads awkwardly
  • ✓Sustained or awkward postures at poorly set-up workstations
  • ✓High-force exertions — tightening bolts, carrying equipment
  • ✓Contact stress — tools pressing against the body during use

🧠 Psychosocial Hazards

Affect psychological health and can cause mental injury. Under updated WHS laws, employers have a positive duty to manage these proactively.

  • ✓Workplace bullying, harassment, or aggression
  • ✓Excessive workload or unrealistic deadlines
  • ✓Lack of role clarity or job control
  • ✓Poor workplace relationships and lack of support
  • ✓Exposure to traumatic events or content

Section 3 — Risk Control

The Hierarchy of Controls

Once you identify a hazard, you need to control the risk. WHS law requires you to apply the most effective control reasonably practicable — using this hierarchy as your guide. Always start at the top.

1. Elimination — Most Effective

Remove the hazard entirely. Example: if a process uses a toxic solvent, reformulate it so the solvent is no longer needed. Always aim for elimination first.

2. Substitution

Replace the hazardous thing with something safer. Example: swap heavy equipment for a lighter model to reduce manual handling risk.

3. Isolation

Separate people from the hazard. Example: install machine guards, use barriers, or lock out equipment during maintenance.

4. Engineering Controls

Physically modify the environment or equipment. Example: install ventilation to reduce dust exposure, or provide a trolley to reduce manual lifting.

5. Administrative Controls

Change how work is done. Example: rotate workers to reduce repetitive strain, create safe work procedures, train staff on hazards.

6. PPE — Least Effective

Personal Protective Equipment should be the last resort, not the first. Hard hats and gloves protect the person but don't reduce the hazard itself. Relying on PPE alone often signals that higher controls haven't been properly implemented.

⚠️
Common mistake: Many workplaces default to PPE because it's cheap and fast. Under WHS law this is not acceptable if higher-order controls are reasonably practicable.

Section 4 — Rights & Responsibilities

What the law requires of you

WHS legislation creates clear duties for everyone in the workplace. Understanding your obligations — and rights — means you can act with confidence.

⚖️

Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Australia)

Adopted across most Australian states and territories. Requires all businesses to eliminate or minimise health and safety risks, so far as is "reasonably practicable." Victoria and Western Australia operate their own equivalent legislation, but the core duties are consistent nationwide.

👷 Your duties as a worker
Under section 28 of the WHS Act, workers must:

• Take reasonable care for their own health and safety
• Take reasonable care that their actions don't adversely affect others
• Comply with reasonable instructions from the PCBU (employer)
• Co-operate with reasonable WHS policies and procedures

Failing these duties can result in prosecution, fines, and in serious cases, imprisonment.
🏢 Employer / PCBU duties
A "Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking" (PCBU) must ensure, so far as reasonably practicable:

• A safe work environment, plant, and structures
• Safe systems of work
• Adequate facilities for workers
• Information, training, instruction, and supervision
• Health monitoring where relevant

PCBUs who breach these duties face fines up to $3 million (corporations) or $300,000 for individuals.
🚫 The right to refuse unsafe work
You have the right to cease work if you have a reasonable concern that it would expose you or others to a serious risk from an immediate hazard.

You must notify your PCBU as soon as practicable. You cannot be dismissed or disadvantaged for exercising this right in good faith — this protection is enshrined in section 84 of the WHS Act.
📋 Notifiable incidents
Certain incidents must be reported to the WHS regulator immediately:

• Deaths, serious injuries or illness (fracture, amputation, loss of consciousness)
• Dangerous incidents — near-misses with serious potential

In Australia, report to your state or territory regulator (e.g. SafeWork NSW, WorkSafe Victoria, SafeWork SA). The scene must also be preserved. Document everything — even near misses.
✅
Best practice: Report even near-misses in your incident register. They are early warnings of more serious incidents — and reporting them protects your colleagues.
